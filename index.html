§ 1

Background: Fourier and the Theory of Heat

In 1807, Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier submitted a manuscript to the French Academy of Sciences presenting a revolutionary mathematical treatment of heat conduction. Rejected initially — Lagrange, Laplace, and Legendre doubted the convergence of his trigonometric series — it was finally published in 1822 as the landmark Théorie analytique de la chaleur (Analytical Theory of Heat).[1] Fourier's central insight was that any temperature distribution, however irregular, could be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal modes — a concept now foundational to all of mathematical physics.

The physical premise is Fourier's law of heat conduction: the heat flux through a material is proportional to the local temperature gradient, directed from hot to cold. For an isotropic material this is expressed as a vector relation,

Fourier's Law: $\mathbf{q} = -k\,\nabla T$ $\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$
where $\mathbf{q}$ is the heat flux vector (W m⁻²), $k$ is the thermal conductivity (W m⁻¹ K⁻¹), and $\nabla T$ is the temperature gradient (K m⁻¹). The negative sign reflects that heat flows against the gradient — from high to low temperature.

Combining Fourier's law with conservation of energy in a control volume yields the heat equation — a parabolic partial differential equation that governs the evolution of temperature in time and space. This equation underpins an enormous range of engineering and scientific disciplines: thermal management of microelectronics, cryopreservation of biological tissue, heat treatment of metals, modeling of geothermal gradients, and the design of spacecraft thermal protection systems.

This simulator solves the one-dimensional heat equation numerically using an explicit finite-difference scheme, allowing real-time exploration of how geometry (coordinate system), material properties, and boundary conditions shape the transient and steady-state temperature distribution.

Historical Note
Fourier's series expansion — expressing an arbitrary function as an infinite sum of sines and cosines — was initially controversial because it appeared to violate the prevailing assumption that only "analytic" functions could arise in physics. It took decades for the convergence theory to be rigorously established, eventually leading to Dirichlet's and Riemann's contributions to real analysis.
§ 2

Mathematical Formulation

Starting from the first law of thermodynamics applied to a differential control volume, the rate of change of internal energy equals the net rate of heat conduction into the element (assuming no internal heat generation and constant properties):

General Form: $\rho\, c_p \dfrac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (k\,\nabla T)$
Simplified (k const): $\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha\,\nabla^2 T$ $\alpha = k\,/\,(\rho\,c_p)$
where $\rho$ (kg m⁻³) is density, $c_p$ (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) is specific heat capacity, and $\alpha$ (m² s⁻¹) is the thermal diffusivity — the ratio of a material's ability to conduct heat to its ability to store it.

For one-dimensional problems, the Laplacian $\nabla^2 T$ takes different forms depending on the coordinate system. Each geometry arises naturally in physical problems:

Cartesian (x): $\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha\dfrac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}$ plane wall, slab
Cylindrical (r): $\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \dfrac{\alpha}{r}\dfrac{\partial}{\partial r}\!\left(r\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right)$ long rod, pipe wall
$= \alpha\!\left(\dfrac{\partial^2 T}{\partial r^2} + \dfrac{1}{r}\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right)$
Spherical (r): $\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \dfrac{\alpha}{r^2}\dfrac{\partial}{\partial r}\!\left(r^2\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right)$ sphere, droplet
$= \alpha\!\left(\dfrac{\partial^2 T}{\partial r^2} + \dfrac{2}{r}\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right)$
The cylindrical and spherical forms are radial ($r\colon 0 \to R$), appropriate when there is angular symmetry. The extra $1/r$ and $2/r$ terms account for the diverging cross-sectional area in each geometry. Cylindrical axial transport (along $z$) reduces to the Cartesian equation.

Steady-state forms. When $\partial T/\partial t = 0$, each equation reduces to an ODE. In Cartesian geometry the steady-state profile is simply linear. In cylindrical geometry it is logarithmic ($T \propto \ln r + C$), and in spherical geometry it is hyperbolic ($T \propto -1/r + C$). These analytical solutions serve as exact benchmarks for validating the numerical solver.

Boundary conditions. The heat equation is a second-order PDE and requires one condition at each spatial boundary. Two physical types are supported:

Dirichlet: $T\big|_\Gamma = T_\text{prescribed}$ fixed temperature (K)
Neumann: $-k\left.\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial n}\right|_\Gamma = q_\text{prescribed}$ fixed heat flux (W m⁻²)
$n$ denotes the outward unit normal. A Neumann value of $q = 0$ describes a perfectly insulated (adiabatic) surface. For radial geometries (cylindrical/radial, spherical), the inner boundary at $r = 0$ must be Neumann with $q = 0$ to enforce the physical symmetry condition $\partial T/\partial r\big|_{r=0} = 0$.

§ 3

Numerical Method

The simulator uses an explicit forward-Euler in time / central-difference in space finite-difference scheme. The spatial domain $[0, L]$ is discretised into $N$ equally spaced nodes with grid spacing $\Delta x = L/(N-1)$. Time is advanced in steps of $\Delta t$.

Discretisation — Cartesian. Applying a first-order forward difference in time and second-order central differences in space to $\partial T/\partial t = \alpha\,\partial^2 T/\partial x^2$ gives:

Interior update: $T_i^{n+1} = T_i^n + r\!\left(T_{i+1}^n - 2T_i^n + T_{i-1}^n\right)$
Mesh ratio: $r = \alpha\,\Delta t\,/\,\Delta x^2$ dimensionless
The superscript $n$ denotes the current time level; $i$ is the spatial index. The scheme is explicit — the new value at each node is computed entirely from known values at the previous time step, requiring no linear system solve for each time step.

Cylindrical and spherical coordinates. Expanding the divergence form of the radial equations and applying central differences to both the second-derivative and first-derivative (curvature) terms:

Cylindrical: $T_i^{n+1} = T_i^n + r\!\left(T_{i+1} - 2T_i + T_{i-1}\right) + \dfrac{\alpha\,\Delta t}{r_i}\cdot\dfrac{T_{i+1} - T_{i-1}}{2\,\Delta x}$
Spherical: $T_i^{n+1} = T_i^n + r\!\left(T_{i+1} - 2T_i + T_{i-1}\right) + \dfrac{2\alpha\,\Delta t}{r_i}\cdot\dfrac{T_{i+1} - T_{i-1}}{2\,\Delta x}$
Here $r_i = i\cdot\Delta x$ is the radial position of node $i$. The additional $1/r$ term (cylindrical) or $2/r$ term (spherical) represents the geometric curvature of the coordinate system. At $r = 0$, L'Hôpital's rule gives the limiting forms shown below.

Origin treatment ($r = 0$). The $1/r_i$ terms are singular at $i = 0$. Applying L'Hôpital's rule and the symmetry condition $\partial T/\partial r\big|_{r=0} = 0$ (equivalently, a ghost-point value $T_{-1} = T_1$) yields:

Cylindrical, r=0: $T_0^{n+1} = T_0^n + 4r\!\left(T_1^n - T_0^n\right)$ factor of 4
Spherical, r=0: $T_0^{n+1} = T_0^n + 6r\!\left(T_1^n - T_0^n\right)$ factor of 6
These limiting coefficients (4 for cylindrical, 6 for spherical) arise from the dimensionality of the Laplacian at the origin: $\lim_{r\to 0}\frac{1}{r^{d-1}}\frac{d}{dr}\!\left(r^{d-1}\frac{dT}{dr}\right) = d\,\frac{d^2T}{dr^2}$ in $d$ spatial dimensions.

Neumann boundary conditions — ghost-point method. For a Neumann BC $-k(\partial T/\partial n)|_\Gamma = q$ at a boundary node, a fictitious ghost node is introduced one grid spacing beyond the boundary. The derivative condition fixes the ghost value, which is substituted into the interior stencil applied at the boundary node:

Left (x=0), ghost: $T_{-1} = T_1 + 2\,\Delta x\cdot q_L/k$ $q_L > 0 \Rightarrow$ heat in
Right (x=L), ghost: $T_N = T_{N-2} - 2\,\Delta x\cdot q_R/k$ $q_R > 0 \Rightarrow$ heat out
Substituting the ghost values into the standard second-order stencil gives a second-order-accurate Neumann update at the boundary — no first-order one-sided differences are needed.

Von Neumann stability analysis. The explicit scheme is conditionally stable. Substituting a Fourier mode $T_i^n = \xi^n e^{ik_i\Delta x}$ into the update equation and requiring the amplification factor $|\xi| \leq 1$ yields:

Cartesian / Cyl. axial: $\Delta t \leq \Delta x^2\,/\,(2\alpha)$ $r \leq 0.5$
Cylindrical radial: $\Delta t \leq \Delta x^2\,/\,(4\alpha)$ $r \leq 0.25$, limited by $r=0$ node
Spherical: $\Delta t \leq \Delta x^2\,/\,(6\alpha)$ $r \leq 1/6$, limited by $r=0$ node
The more restrictive limits for radial geometries arise from the larger coefficients at the $r = 0$ origin node (factor 4 and 6 vs. 2 in Cartesian). The simulator automatically enforces these limits and warns when the requested $\Delta t$ exceeds the maximum stable value.

Steady-state solver — Thomas algorithm. Setting $\partial T/\partial t = 0$ converts the PDE to a system of linear algebraic equations with a tridiagonal coefficient matrix. This is solved exactly in $O(N)$ operations using the Thomas algorithm (tridiagonal matrix algorithm), providing the analytical steady-state solution as an overlay on the transient simulation. In Cartesian geometry the steady state is a linear profile; in cylindrical geometry it is logarithmic; in spherical geometry it is of the form $T = A - B/r$.

Stability Note
The explicit scheme requires $r \leq r_\text{max}$ for numerical stability. If the requested $\Delta t$ exceeds the maximum stable value, the simulator automatically caps $\Delta t$ at 99.9% of the stability limit. A badge in the status bar indicates whether the simulation is running at the requested or a reduced time step.
